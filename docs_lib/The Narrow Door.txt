The Narrow Door
Others might have turned this story into a book but the tale I set down here I lived with all my strength and my virtue was worn away in it I shall write my memories very simply and though they lie in tattered fragments here and there I will not invent anything to piece or join them up the trouble I might take to smooth them over would spoil the last pleasure I hope to find in telling them

I was not yet twelve when I lost my father My mother who had no more ties to Le Havre where my father had practised as a doctor resolved to move to Paris thinking I should finish my studies better there She rented a small apartment near the Luxembourg Gardens and Miss Flora Ashburton came to live with us Miss Ashburton who had no family left had first been my mother’s governess then her companion and soon her dearest friend I grew up between these two women both gentle and melancholy who in all my earliest recollections wore nothing but mourning black

One day long after my father’s death I noticed a pale violet ribbon replacing the black one on my mother’s bonnet and cried out in surprise
Oh Mother that colour suits you dreadfully

The next day she had put the black ribbon back again

I was a delicate child My mother and Miss Ashburton took endless care not to tire me and luckily I loved learning so they did not spoil me into idleness Whenever the weather turned mild they thought my cheeks grew too pale and that I needed to escape the city so every year about the middle of June we left for Fonguesmar a farm on the outskirts of Le Havre where my uncle Bucolin lived and who received us every summer

The Bucolins’ garden was neither large nor handsome far less striking than most Norman gardens The white three storey house stood facing east with twenty tall windows opening on to the garden front and back only blank walls to left and right The panes were small squares of glass some newly replaced ones shone bright against the dull grey green old panes and a few were flawed with the bubbles old folk called air eyes through which trees looked warped and the postman passing by seemed suddenly hunchbacked

The garden was an oblong plot enclosed by walls A wide lawn stretched before the house ringed by a gravel path The wall facing the dwelling was low letting one see the farmyard beyond An alley of hornbeams ran along the right hand wall and a fine fruit orchard spread to the left At the far end a small wooden summerhouse stood beneath a tall birch tree which we called the pavilion

My uncle Bucolin was a quiet easy going man worn down by sorrows His wife my aunt had died young leaving him three children Alissa Juliette and a boy named Georges who was younger than Juliette and about my own age I spent all my days running about with the cousins though Alissa was two years older than me and her quiet gravity kept her somewhat apart from our noisy games

I shall never forget the afternoon when my life was utterly changed I had wandered back to the house after a long walk and as I pushed open the door to my aunt’s bedroom I found Alissa kneeling beside the bed her back to the window where a thin streak of sunset light filtered through She turned her head when she heard me draw near but did not rise only murmured
Jerome why have you come back

I knelt down beside her and kissed her forehead her cheeks were streaming with tears I did not fully grasp the cause of her grief then yet I felt with terrible intensity that such overwhelming sorrow could not be borne by her trembling young soul her fragile body shaken by sobs I stood beside her as she knelt unable to put into words the strange tumult surging inside me but I pressed her head to my chest laid my lips to her brow and poured all my soul into that touch Drunk with love and pity tangled in a confused rush of devotion self sacrifice and pure goodness I cried out to God with all my heart I offered myself wholly to Him unable to imagine any purpose to life save shielding this girl from fear from evil from all the harshness of the world At last I knelt beside her wrapped her gently in my arms and faintly heard her whisper

Jerome They did not see you did they Go away quickly they must not find you here

Then her voice dropped even lower
Jerome do not tell anyone Poor Papa knows nothing of this

I told my mother nothing of what I had seen yet the endless whispered conversations between her and Aunt Plantier the women’s worried preoccupied glances the constant Run along dear child that sent me away whenever they spoke together made it plain that some secret sorrow hung over the Bucolin household

Later I learned that my aunt Lucile Uncle Bucolin’s wife had not always been the quiet melancholy woman I remembered In her youth she had been wild flighty careless of appearances and had brought great shame to her family before her marriage Though her husband bore her frailties patiently her restlessness never faded she slipped away from the house often wandered alone along the coast and fell into reckless friendships It was one such secret meeting that Alissa had stumbled upon that afternoon the source of her bitter weeping

Lucile Bucolin rarely joined our family gatherings she would stay shut in her room until midday dinner then sink straight on to a sofa or hammock and lie there until evening rising only as listless as before Sometimes she pressed a fine linen handkerchief to her forehead as if wiping sweat from skin smooth and unblemished the cloth carried a soft fruity scent unlike any flower perfume She kept a tiny silver cased mirror tucked in her waistband which she would pull out to glance at herself for minutes on end her gaze distant and empty

Uncle Bucolin endured all this without reproach He loved his wife deeply even after her heart had wandered away from him The whole household walked on eggshells around Lucile afraid to stir her anger or send her rushing off to the cliffs alone Alissa as the eldest daughter bore the heaviest burden of this silent shame it fell to her to watch over her mother to smooth over scenes to hide her mother’s absences from the neighbours and to comfort her father when his heart ached That day finding her mother’s secret grief laid bare Alissa’s young heart had broken entirely

From that evening onward a strange sacred bond bound Alissa and me together I did not yet understand the shape of the love growing in me only that every thought every hope I owned revolved around her I longed to share her burden to take her sorrow upon myself to walk beside her along the narrow path she already dreamed of treading the strait gate spoken of in the Gospels the gate that few would strive hard enough to enter

We spent hours reading the Bible side by side in the pavilion at the end of the garden Alissa loved the Gospels above all else and she would read aloud the words of Christ in a soft trembling voice as if she spoke straight to God We pondered the parable of the narrow gate together and even as children we both understood its terrible demand to cast aside all trivial earthly joy all selfish longing to strip oneself bare before heaven if one hoped to pass through

Juliette Alissa’s younger sister was warmer lighter more eager for simple happiness She loved our games laughed freely and could not grasp Alissa’s hunger for spiritual purity Georges the boy cared only for hunting and sailing along the coast and paid little mind to our quiet readings So Alissa and I were left alone in our shared reverence our shared longing for a holiness that felt almost out of reach

Summer slipped away and we returned to Paris Every letter I sent to Fonguesmar was filled with thoughts of Alissa every reply from her became a treasure I hid beneath my pillow The months apart only deepened the quiet devotion between us a love that dared not speak its name already yielding to the shadow of sacrifice that would divide us in the years to come
