Heidi
By Johanna Spyri
Chapter 1 Up the Mountain to Alm Uncle
It surprises me beyond words that you can think of doing such a thing Dete said Barbel in a voice full of reproach
What do you mean retorted Dete I have done my duty by the child and what would you have me do with it now I cannot certainly take a child of five years old with me to Frankfurt But where are you going to yourself Barbel we are now half way up the Alm
We have just reached the place I wanted answered Barbel I had something to say to the goatherds wife who does some spinning for me in the winter So good bye Dete and good luck to you
Dete shook hands with her friend and remained standing while Barbel went towards a small dark brown hut which stood a few steps away from the path in a hollow that afforded it some protection from the mountain wind The hut was situated half way up the Alm reckoning from Dorfli and it was well that it was provided with some shelter for it was so broken down and dilapidated that even then it must have been very unsafe as a habitation for when the stormy south wind came sweeping over the mountain everything inside it doors and windows shook and rattled and all the rotten old beams creaked and trembled On such days as this had the goatherds dwelling been standing alone upon the open hillside it would long ago have been blown flat
Dete sighed and looked after Barbel then she lifted the little girl who walked beside her by the hand and went further up the mountain
Heidi was a little child of five with thick curly brown hair bright eyes and cheeks as red as wild strawberries She wore a thin faded dress and a shawl wrapped loosely around her shoulders In her small arms she held a bundle containing all her few belongings
The two climbed steadily higher the air growing sharper and cooler as they left the village far below The path wound past green meadows dotted with wild flowers and tall pine trees whose branches swayed loudly in the wind
After a long walk they came within sight of a lonely wooden hut perched high on the mountainside surrounded by great fir trees This was the home of Alm Uncle Heidis grandfather
Dete slowed her steps as they drew near for she felt uneasy about leaving the little girl here She had cared for Heidi ever since the childs parents had passed away but a well paid job in Frankfurt waited for her and she could not turn it down
The old man sat outside his hut on a rough wooden bench smoking a long pipe His thick grey eyebrows hung heavy over his eyes and a huge white beard covered his chin He did not look up as they approached
Good day uncle Dete called out stepping forward I have brought Heidi to stay with you
The old man raised his head and fixed his sharp gaze upon the woman and the small child beside her What brings you here he asked in a rough cold voice
I cannot keep the child any longer Dete explained I have looked after her these four years since her mother my sister died Now I have work far away and you are her only living relative It falls to you to take care of her
That is no concern of mine the old man replied sharply I want no child here Take her away with you
I cannot do that Dete said firmly If you refuse to keep her you must make other arrangements No one else in the village will take her in and you will bear all blame if any harm comes to her
The old man stared at Heidi who stood quietly watching him without fear Her bright eyes met his steadily and she did not shrink back
When the child begins to cry and pine for you what shall I do with her then he demanded
That is your trouble Dete answered I endured all her tears and trouble when she was an infant and I had my own mother to care for as well Now it is your turn
The old man said nothing more but turned his gaze back to the mountains
Dete quickly set Heidis small bundle down on the ground beside the bench leaned down to whisper a hasty goodbye to the little girl and hurried back down the path without another word She dared not linger for fear her heart would soften and she would give up her new job
Heidi stood alone before the stranger watching him silently The old man took slow puffs of his pipe and did not glance at her for a long while
At last he turned his head and saw her still standing there What do you want he asked gruffly
Heidi took a small step forward and said I want to know where I shall sleep
The old man rose from his bench and walked into the hut without speaking Heidi followed close behind him
Inside the hut everything was plain and simple One large wooden table three stools a cupboard for food and clothes and a narrow bed in the corner made up all the furniture Up under the sloped roof there was a wide pile of clean straw with a thick linen cover spread over it
That will be your sleeping place the old man said pointing to the straw heap
Heidi climbed up at once and laid herself down on the soft straw looking out through a small open window that faced the green mountain slopes and distant snow peaks She smiled happily for she had never slept anywhere so lovely before
When evening came the grandfather brought her a bowl of fresh goats milk and a thick slice of dark bread Heidi ate heartily and felt warm and content despite the old mans harsh quiet manner
As darkness fell she curled up on her straw bed and listened to the soft rustle of the fir trees outside the hut already knowing in her heart that she would grow to love this lonely mountain home and her silent grandfather
