Wuthering Heights
By Emily Bronte
1801 I have just returned from a visit to my landlord the solitary neighbour that I shall be troubled with This is certainly a beautiful country In all England I do not believe that I can fix on a situation so completely removed from the stir of society A perfect misanthropists heaven and Mr Heathcliff and I are such a suitable pair to divide the desolation between us A capital fellow He little imagined how my heart warmed towards him when I beheld his black eyes withdraw so suspiciously under their brows as I rode up and when his fingers sheltered themselves with a jealous resolution still further in his waistcoat as I announced my name
Mr Heathcliff I said A nod was the answer
Mr Lockwood your new tenant sir I come to do myself the honour of calling as soon as possible after my arrival to express the hope that I have not inconvenienced you by my perseverance in soliciting the occupation of Thrushcross Grange I heard yesterday you had some thoughts
Thrushcross Grange is my own sir he interrupted wincing I should not allow any one to inconvenience me if I could hinder it walk in
The walk in was uttered with closed teeth and expressed the sentiment Go to the deuce even the gate over which he leant manifested no sympathising movement to the words and I think that circumstance determined me to accept the invitation I felt interested in a man who seemed more exaggeratedly reserved than myself
When he had opened the wooden gate and we both passed in he observed There is my house within the bar and he stretched out his hand with the intention of shutting it but I anticipated him
I admire your prejudice I remarked I should not like to be enclosed if I were in your place without delay I grasped the bolt He hesitated a moment and then added I have few people here so the gate is not often kept closed
A noble dog doubtless I responded glancing at a large greyhound whose head rested on her paws watching my motions narrowly as I spoke
You are not afraid of dogs he said throwing open the inner door with a grim smile
I am afraid of nothing I answered
A cur came forth snarling at his heels and three others followed to reinforce her threats
Keep to the house muttered Heathcliff and the dogs though I had no intention of disturbing their repose I made no movement till they had retreated Then I advanced to the hearth
The room was simply furnished a heavy wooden table several rough chairs a large stone fireplace and bare stone walls No ornaments or fine fabrics softened the harsh air of the place A few guns hung above the mantel and a collection of hides lay piled in one corner
Mr Heathcliff drew a stool to the fire and bade me sit I took the seat and he filled a large mug with ale pushing it toward me before settling himself on another stool opposite
I have not yet seen the full extent of your grounds I said after a short silence the view from the Grange must be far more pleasant
Thrushcross Grange stands in a hollow sheltered by trees he replied coldly It is soft and gentle country fit for weak spirits This place Wuthering Heights sits exposed on the hill catching every wind that sweeps the moor the name itself speaks of the wild weather that beats against its walls
The house bore marks of long exposure to storm thick stone walls small deep set windows and heavy wooden shutters fastened with iron bars Every timber and beam looked worn and weathered by years of harsh mountain winds
I glanced about the room and noticed no signs of other inhabitants No female belongings no childrens toys only masculine rough worn goods laid out plainly
Do you live entirely alone here I asked
My household is small he said I have one servant an old man who tends to the fire and the cattle No wife no children no kin to share the space
I wondered at the loneliness of such an existence yet his stern face gave no hint of sorrow or longing only hard unyielding calm
We talked little more he answered my questions in short abrupt sentences and showed no wish to prolong my visit I finished my ale and rose to take my leave
I shall not trouble you long with frequent calls I said I intend to spend most of my days quietly at the Grange reading and walking across the moor
He nodded and walked with me to the gate where the dogs still lingered watching my departure
I mounted my horse and rode away casting one last look back at Wuthering Heights standing dark and lonely on the bare hill under a grey sky The wild moor stretched endless all around broken only by low stones and thin wind bent shrubs and I thought that never had I met a man so wrapped in cold isolation as Mr Heathcliff
