The Red and the Black
By Stendhal
A small town in the foothills of the Jura mountains named Verrieres stands against a slope covered with white stone quarries and thousands of workers cutting rock every day The noise of hammers echoes across the hills from dawn till dusk and the town survives entirely on the stone trade
Every street in Verrieres is lined with rough stone houses built from local rock and the whole place carries an air of busy narrow minded wealth Its mayor Monsieur de Renal owns the finest residence in town a large stone mansion surrounded by a wide garden enclosed by high walls planted with chestnut trees He holds the most respected position in the district and all residents bow to him in public
One spring morning Monsieur de Renal stood at the iron gate of his home with a dark expression watching three labourers carry heavy stone slabs into his garden
I told you to delay this work until autumn he shouted at the head worker You ruin my lawn with your carts and heavy loads and the young leaves on my chestnut trees get crushed under your wheels
Sir the worker replied we have no choice the stone we need for your new terrace can only be moved safely when the ground is dry and the rain will turn these paths to mud in a few weeks
Monsieur de Renal frowned and tapped his walking stick against the iron bars of the gate He cared more about the appearance of his garden than any trouble the workers faced He had spent a great deal of gold expanding his grounds to show off his status to every passerby
The town’s main source of income besides stone cutting was a textile factory run by a clever self made man named Valenod He collected taxes from the poor workers and grew richer each year while his labourers lived in cramped cold rooms on the edge of Verrieres
A priest called Father Chelan walked slowly past the mayor’s gate He was an old kind man who had lived in Verrieres for thirty years and all townsfolk trusted his judgment Monsieur de Renal forced a polite smile and greeted him though he secretly disliked the priest’s habit of speaking up for the poor against wealthy men
The priest stopped and glanced at the half finished terrace in the garden You spend much money on stone and greenery sir he said quietly but you give little help to the hungry families in the lower district
Monsieur de Renal’s face turned stiff He changed the subject quickly and asked about the new young tutor the priest had recommended for his three children
His name is Julien Sorel Father Chelan said He is the son of a carpenter from the edge of town He reads well and memorises religious texts perfectly and he can guide your boys through their studies
The mayor thought for a moment He needed someone to teach his sons Latin and moral lessons without charging an excessive wage A carpenter’s son would not demand high pay and would obey his orders without argument
Send the boy to my house tomorrow morning Monsieur de Renal told the priest I will test his knowledge and decide if he suits my family
Father Chelan nodded and continued walking toward the poor quarter of the town
Inside the mayor’s large house Madame de Renal sat by the window sewing soft linen cloth for her children She was gentle and kind and often worried about the harsh gaps between rich and poor in Verrieres She did not share her husband’s obsession with public prestige and grand gardens
When Monsieur de Renal entered the room he told her of the new tutor he planned to hire
A carpenter’s boy she asked Will he treat our sons gently and speak proper language in front of us
He has studied under the priest the mayor answered He knows all the scripture and will keep the boys away from foolish idle talk I will pay him a small room and a little food each month no more
Madame de Renal hoped the young tutor would bring calm soft influence to her three restless sons
Far outside the town Julien Sorel sat on a rock beside his father’s carpentry shop He was thin pale with large dark eyes and he held a worn copy of a religious book close to his chest He hated the loud rough work of cutting wood and carrying planks for his father and dreamed constantly of escaping the dull narrow life of a labourer
His father shouted at him from the workshop ordering him to carry a pile of timber to the storage shed Julien closed his book slowly and stood up his heart heavy with distaste for the hard meaningless work ahead He did not know yet that the invitation to serve as tutor at the mayor’s house would twist the whole course of his young life and lead him into a tangled world of wealth secret feeling and dangerous ambition
